A Site That (Better) Represents Me

My previous portfolio was functional but flat — a simple grid that showed projects without much character. It didn't communicate how I work or the kinds of experiences I aim to create through my work. For me, it's obvious how a creator (whether writer, coder, or analyst) works from the moment you come into visual contact with their work — long before you scroll to an About page. My old site failed that test.


Like many of us, I leaned on fast, out-of-the-box tools to get something online quickly when I realized I needed to share my work for jobs and conversations. For me, that tool was Quarto; especially attractive because of its rising popularity among people working at the intersection of data science, analytics, research, and storytelling (and its tight connection to R).


That first site served its purpose, but it wasn't enough. I needed a portfolio that would do two things at once:

  • Showcase my skills in frontend development, animation, and design thinking
  • Express my perspective as a creative technologist — blending narrative, interaction, and code into something cohesive.

The redesign became a chance to treat my own portfolio like a client project: setting goals, experimenting with design direction, and building an experience that reflects who I am now, not who I was when I first coded a site.

Shaping the Direction

The redesign wasn't just a reskin — it was a rebuild. My old site relied on Quarto, a fast, out-of-the-box solution popular among data practitioners. It worked, but it also limited my ability to control design, interaction, and identity. Here's the shift I captured in the redesign:


            Old Portfolio ──> Functional | Fast-build | Tool-dependent (Quarto)
                                  │
                                  ▼
            New Portfolio ──> Interactive | Narrative | Hand-coded Identity

From there, I approached the rebuild as both designer and developer, making deliberate choices about aesthetic direction, interaction design, and the underlying architecture.
